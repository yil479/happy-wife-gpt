# Argument: Division of household chores

**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Trigger:** Sink full of dishes after a long work week.

## What happened
I came home exhausted and found the kitchen exactly how I'd left it that morning — dishes
piled up, laundry still in the dryer. I said something like "does it ever occur to you to
just do this without me asking?" which came out sharper than I meant it to. He said he'd
been slammed with a work deadline and felt like I was keeping score instead of noticing he'd
handled the kids' pickup and dinner prep all week.

## How we felt
I felt invisible, like the "invisible labor" of noticing what needs doing always falls on me.
He felt like nothing he does is ever enough, and that I only see the one thing that's missing
instead of the five things that got done.

## How it was resolved
We didn't resolve it that night — I went for a walk to cool down. The next morning we sat
down and made a real list of recurring chores and assigned "owners" instead of assuming
things get split evenly by default. We agreed to do a 10-minute Sunday check-in on what's
coming up that week (school events, work deadlines) so neither of us is blindsided.

## Reflection
The actual fight wasn't about dishes. It was about feeling unseen versus feeling
unappreciated. Naming that out loud helped more than any chore chart did.
