# Active listening during conflict

Active listening is the practice of fully concentrating on what your partner is saying rather
than preparing your rebuttal while they talk. During an argument, most people listen to
respond, not to understand — and partners can usually tell the difference.

## Core techniques
- **Reflect before reacting.** Paraphrase what you heard ("So you're saying you felt
  blindsided when...") before adding your own perspective. This confirms you understood
  correctly and signals that you're engaged, not just waiting for your turn.
- **Ask before assuming.** If your partner's tone or word choice feels like an attack, ask
  what they meant instead of responding to the worst-case interpretation.
- **Separate venting from problem-solving.** Many conflicts escalate because one partner wants
  to be heard and the other jumps straight to fixing it. A simple check-in — "do you want me
  to help solve this or just listen?" — avoids that mismatch entirely.
- **Don't interrupt to defend yourself.** Let your partner finish their point even if you
  disagree. You will get your turn, and interrupting almost always makes the other person feel
  unheard, which escalates rather than resolves the conflict.

## Why it works
Feeling heard lowers physiological arousal (heart rate, cortisol) during conflict. Most
disagreements aren't really about the surface topic — dishes, money, in-laws — they're about
whether each partner feels seen and valued. Active listening addresses that underlying need
directly, even before any practical solution is reached.
