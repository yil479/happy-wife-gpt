# Repair attempts (Gottman method)

Relationship researcher John Gottman found that the difference between couples who stay
happily together and those who don't isn't whether they fight — it's whether "repair
attempts" during a fight succeed. A repair attempt is any statement or action, serious or
silly, meant to de-escalate tension before it spirals.

## Examples of repair attempts
- "Can we start this over?"
- "I'm sorry, that came out harsher than I meant."
- Humor that lightens the mood without dismissing the issue.
- A hand reaching for the other person's hand mid-argument.
- "I need a few minutes to calm down, can we pick this back up at 8?"
- "I hear you saying this is really important to you."

## Why repair attempts fail
Gottman's research found repair attempts often fail not because they're bad attempts, but
because the *timing* and *state of the relationship* make them hard to receive. If one partner
is already flooded (in a state of fight-or-flight), even a well-meant repair attempt can be
missed or rejected. This is why taking a real break — 20+ minutes, long enough for
physiological arousal to actually come down — before trying to repair matters more than the
specific words used.

## The 5:1 ratio
Gottman also found that stable relationships maintain roughly five positive interactions for
every negative one, even during conflict. This doesn't mean avoiding disagreement — it means
that outside of active conflict, small positive interactions (appreciation, affection, shared
humor) build the reserve of goodwill that makes repair attempts land when they're needed.
