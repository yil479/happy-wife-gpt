# The five love languages and recurring conflict

Gary Chapman's "five love languages" framework describes five broad ways people tend to give
and receive affection: words of affirmation, quality time, receiving gifts, acts of service,
and physical touch. The framework is popular not because it's rigorously scientific, but
because it gives couples a shared vocabulary for a common source of recurring conflict:
partners expressing love in the way *they* prefer to receive it, rather than the way their
partner actually experiences it.

## How this shows up in arguments
A partner whose primary language is acts of service may feel loved when their partner does
the dishes without being asked, and unloved by verbal compliments alone — while their partner,
whose language is words of affirmation, may not register unspoken chores as affection at all
and instead feel a growing distance neither of them can name. This mismatch often gets
mislabeled as "he doesn't help enough" or "she never says anything nice," when the deeper
issue is a language gap, not a lack of care.

## Using it constructively
- Identify your own top one or two languages and say them out loud to your partner — most
  people have never been asked directly.
- Notice what your partner already does that might be their way of expressing love, even if
  it's not how you'd choose to express it.
- Don't use the framework as a scorecard ("well that wasn't really quality time"). It's meant
  to build understanding, not become a new rule to enforce during a fight.
