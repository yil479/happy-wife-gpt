# "I" statements and fair fighting

How a disagreement is expressed often matters more than what it's about. "You" statements
("You never help around here") tend to trigger defensiveness because they sound like an
accusation about character. "I" statements ("I feel overwhelmed when the kitchen piles up and
I'm the one who ends up cleaning it") describe your own experience without putting your
partner on trial.

## The basic structure
1. **I feel** [emotion] — name the actual feeling, not a judgment disguised as a feeling
   ("I feel like you don't care" is a judgment; "I feel hurt" is a feeling).
2. **when** [specific behavior, not character] — describe the concrete situation, not a
   sweeping pattern ("always," "never").
3. **because** [the need or value underneath] — this is often the part people skip, but it's
   what makes the statement land as information rather than attack.
4. **I would like** [a specific, actionable request] — vague complaints are hard to act on;
   specific requests are easy to say yes to.

## Rules for fighting fair
- No character attacks ("you're so lazy," "you're just like your mother").
- No kitchen-sinking — bringing up five unrelated old grievances in one argument.
- No stonewalling — shutting down and refusing to engage is its own form of escalation.
- Take a break if either partner is flooded (heart racing, can't think clearly) and agree on a
  specific time to come back to the conversation — an open-ended "we'll talk later" often
  means never.
- Aim to understand before you aim to win. The goal of a disagreement between partners isn't a
  verdict, it's a shared understanding.
